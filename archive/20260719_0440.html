Assessment of land potential for food crop development in the karst landscape of Gunungsewu hills, IndonesiaCORE4
Authors: Afifatul Husna Al Adilah, Bayu Dwi Apri Nugroho, J. Sartohadi, Edwin Maulana, Dimas Maula Hayat
Venue: GEOGRAPHY, ENVIRONMENT, SUSTAINABILITY
Published: 2026-07-12
Abs · PDF
Abstract
Gunungkidul region in Indonesia, though considered arid and agriculturally limited, has shown notable productivity in food crops, especially upland rice, cassava, groundnut, and maize cultivation. Main challenges of agricultural development in this region are limited accessibility to land due to its hilly and steep topography, as well as water scarcity. Accurate land-use planning tools are needed to allocate land resources to support regional agricultural activity. Therefore, a systematic assessment of land potential for food crop agriculture is needed using geoinformation techniques to enable well-planned development. This study segmented research area into doline units as mapping units. Accessibility, soil, and water factors were employed in evaluating land potential classes. The Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) method was used to determine the weights of parameters and parameter classes scores for each factor. Potential of dolines for food crop agriculture in Gunungkidul was assessed using 10 parameters including distance from road, distance from settlements, area of doline’s valley, rasio of valley’s area and doline’s area, soil depth, soil texture, pH, Soil Organic Matter (SOM), Cation Exchange Capacity (CEC), and uppermost rock. Among these parameters, distance from roads (weight: 0.26), distance from settlements (weight: 0.20), and doline valley area (weight: 0.14) were identified as the most significant accessibility factors. Results indicated that 52% of the karst and karstic areas of Gunungkidul, characterized by arid hills, have potential for food crop agriculture development. This study provides valuable insights for policymakers and stakeholders aiming to promote sustainable agricultural systems in karst landscapes more broadly in arid regions.
Summary / 总结
The study was motivated by the need for accurate land-use planning in the arid and topographically challenging Gunungkidul region of Indonesia, where food crop production (upland rice, cassava, groundnut, maize) is notable despite limited accessibility and water scarcity. The assessment revealed that 52% of the karst and karstic areas have potential for food crop agriculture development, providing guidance for sustainable agricultural systems in similar arid karst landscapes.

Effect Of Dolomite Lime Dosage And Goat Manure Fertilizer Dosage On The Growth And Yield Of Tomato Plant (Lycopersicum esculentum MILL.)CORE3
Authors: Sigit Permono, Luluk Sulistiyo Budi, Sri Rahayu, Ma'ruf Pambudi Nurwantara
Venue: JALOW | Journal of Agribusiness and Local Wisdom
Published: 2026-07-12
Abs · PDF
Abstract
This study evaluated the interaction between dolomite lime and goat manure dosages on the growth and yield of tomato plants (Lycopersicum esculentum Mill.). The experiment was arranged in a factorial randomized block design with two factors and three replications. Dolomite lime was applied at four levels (0, 5, 10, and 15 g per polybag), while goat manure was applied at 0, 37.5, 75, and 112.5 g per polybag. Observations included soil pH, plant height, number of leaves, leaf area, number of branches, number of fruits per plant, and biomass parameters. Significant interactions between dolomite lime and goat manure were observed for leaf number at 36 and 43 days after transplanting (DAT), leaf area at 43 DAT, number of branches at 36 DAT, and number of fruits per plant at 57 DAT, whereas no interaction occurred for soil pH, plant height, or biomass variables. Dolomite lime improved soil chemical conditions by increasing soil pH, while goat manure enhanced vegetative growth through improved nutrient availability. Optimal combinations differed among parameters. Overall, integrated application of dolomite lime and goat manure effectively enhanced tomato growth and yield under acidic soil conditions.

Microplastics in agricultural soils, their sources, environmental impacts, ecological risks, and mitigation strategiesECO1
Authors: M. Kundu
Venue: Discover Environment
Published: 2026-07-12

Abstract
Microplastic contamination in agricultural soils is a rising problem that risks soil health, crop production, and the general balance of nature. These tiny fragments alter soil structural composition and nutrient cycles while also inhibiting microbial processes, which are, without a doubt, the most vital for soil fertility. The research provides data on the origins and the behaviour of microplastics (MPs) in agroecosystems and tracks their movement through biosolids, plastic mulches, and irrigation. Their binding to soil elements changes porosity, aggregation, water retention, and nutrient availability; at the same time, it also increases the risk of transport of heavy metals, pesticides, and pathogens. The solutions on the table include organic amendments, biodegradable products, phytoremediation, and integrated nutrient management to address contaminant–nutrient relationships. Strengthening soil monitoring systems and advancing circular bioeconomy strategies are the major steps toward alleviating plastic pollution in rural areas. A unified strategy that integrates scientific research, policy measures, and sustainable farming practices is crucial for reducing microplastic pollution, enhancing soil resilience, and promoting the long-term sustainability of agricultural ecosystems.

Abstract
Land degradation is defined as the persistent reduction in the capacity of land to support humans and other life on Earth. Intensified land use transformation from natural to sealed surfaces and intensive management practices accelerates the pace of land degradation. Several land‐use changes constitute potential drivers of degradation; additional pressures, including intensive tillage, pesticide applications, unsustainable irrigation, and excessive fertilizer use, further compromise soil resilience and undermine conservation efforts. The agricultural sector and the natural resources systems face heightened threats such as loss from erosion, decreasing soil organic carbon stocks, and increasing soil salinization due to poor irrigation water use. In addition to on‐site adverse effects on soil, water, and air resources, these processes exert off‐site negative impacts on socioeconomically vulnerable communities. Over recent decades, climate change, driven by anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions, has intensified pressures on terrestrial ecosystems through rising temperatures and increased frequency of weather extremes, thereby accelerating land degradation processes. Primary research and systematic evidence synthesis are needed to better address land degradation in response to the combinations of climate change drivers and ongoing human activities. In this editorial, we will provide a comprehensive overview of the anthropogenic impacts on land degradation highlighting the need for integrated approaches that address the social, economic, and environmental dimensions of this critical issue. This editorial draws on a range of studies and examples from around the world, outlining maily on contributions to this special issue “Anthropogenic Impacts on Land Degradation in a Changing World” and other LDD papers, to demonstrate the complexity and urgency of the land degradation issue. We will also emphasize the importance of addressing land degradation in the context of the SDGs, particularly SDG 15.3.1, and the need for a coordinated and integrated approach to addressing this issue.
Summary / 总结
This editorial was motivated by the growing threat of anthropogenic impacts on land degradation in a changing climate, emphasizing the need for integrated solutions. The method involved a comprehensive synthesis of studies from the special issue and other Land Degradation & Development papers, reviewing global examples and drivers. Key findings highlight that intensified land use conversion to sealed surfaces, along with intensive tillage, pesticide application, unsustainable irrigation, and excessive fertilizer use, accelerate degradation, resulting in erosion, soil organic carbon loss, and salinization. These processes also produce off-site negative effects on socioeconomically vulnerable communities. Climate change, through rising temperatures and weather extremes, further intensifies these pressures. The editorial underscores the urgency of addressing land degradation within the framework of the Sustainable Development Goals, particularly SDG 15.3.1, and calls for coordinated approaches that consider social, economic, and environmental dimensions.
